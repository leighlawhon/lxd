1890

William James publishes "Principles of Psychology," which lays some groundwork for the study of behavior.

1898

Edward Thorndike conducts his experiments on animal learning, leading to the development of his Law of Effect, which states that behaviors followed by satisfying consequences are more likely to be repeated.

1904

Ivan Pavlov wins the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for his work on digestive processes, during which he discovers classical conditioning.

1903

John B. Watson publishes "Psychology as the Behaviorist Views It," often considered the formal founding of behaviorism. He argues that psychology should focus on observable behavior, not internal mental states.

1920

Watson and Rosalie Rayner conduct the "Little Albert" experiment, demonstrating classical conditioning in humans by conditioning a young child to fear a white rat.

1930s

B.F. Skinner begins his work on operant conditioning, conducting experiments with rats and pigeons using the Skinner box to study how behavior is influenced by reinforcement and punishment.

1938

Skinner publishes "The Behavior of Organisms," which outlines the principles of operant conditioning and introduces key concepts such as reinforcement schedules.

1943

SClark Hull publishes "Principles of Behavior," which attempts to explain learning and behavior in terms of mathematical formulas and hypotheses.

1953

kinner publishes "Science and Human Behavior," which applies the principles of behaviorism to human behavior and society.

1960s

Behaviorism begins to be challenged by cognitive psychology, which focuses on internal mental processes such as thinking, memory, and problem-solving.

1971

Skinner publishes "Beyond Freedom and Dignity," advocating for the application of behaviorist principles to solve social issues.
